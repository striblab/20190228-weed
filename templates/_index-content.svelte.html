Marijuana's changing landscape

By Jeff Hargarten and Matt DeLong • Star Tribune

As recreational marijuana gains legal acceptance throughout the nation state-by-state, Minnesota lawmakers are mulling the drug's status.

Gov. Tim Walz has expressed openness to legalizing marijuana for recreational use, following the lead of states like Colorado, Washington, Oregon, California and others.

Marijuana's legal status in Minnesota is complex. On one hand, cannabis has been decriminalized for small doses in Minnesota since the 1970s and available as a treatment for terminal medical conditions since 2015. On the other hand, large racial disparities in enforcement persist statewide and the medical availability of marijuana-based treatments is among the country's most restrictive.

Here's a rundown of where Minnesota stands on marijuana's legality:

The greening of America

More than half the country has legalized marijuana in some form. Several others have made cannabis-based oils available to treat various medical conditions. A growing number have allowed marijuana to be sold for recreational use from licensed businesses.

Growing revenue

To states that have legalized marijuana for recreational sales, the additional tax revenue has been a boon for local budgets. Though since marijuana is still federally considered a prohibited Schedule One drug, legal businesses selling cannabis face various operational challenges.

Minnesota's marijuana limitations

Minnesota has one of the nation’s strictest medical marijuana programs, allowing only seriously ill patients to ingest concentrated marijuana in edible or vapor form, but prohibits smoking.

Qualifying conditions include cancer, glaucoma, HIV/AIDS, Tourette Syndrome, ALS, seizures, muscle spasms, Multiple Sclerosis, Chrohn's Disease and any illness deemed terminal.

Cannabis-based treatments are only legally available from twelve licensed dispensaries serving all of Minnesota's 87 counties.

Recreationally, small amounts of marijuana in personal possession have been decriminalized in Minnesota since 1976. Any weight less 42.5 grams or less is a petty misdemeanor on first offense, while future offenses are considered felonies.

Changing attitudes

While acceptance of legalized marijuana has gradually increased nationwide over the decade, support in the midwest has grown more inconsistently, yet still jumped about 25 points from 2009 to 2018.

Continued enforcement

The number of marijuana convictions in Minnesota have continued to rise in the last several years.

Continuing racial disparities

Blacks and Native Americans represent disproportionate percentages of cannabis cases filed in Minnesota relative to their overall percentage of the population, while whites are under-represented.
