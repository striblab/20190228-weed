Where Minnesota stands on marijuana legalization

By Jeff Hargarten and Matt DeLong • Star Tribune

As recreational marijuana gradually gains legal acceptance throughout the nation, Minnesota lawmakers are mulling the drug's status.

Gov. Tim Walz has expressed openness to legalizing marijuana for recreational use, following the lead of states like Colorado, Washington, Oregon, California and others.

Minnesota's relationship with marijuana is complex. On one hand, cannabis has been decriminalized for small doses in Minnesota since the 1970s and available as a treatment for terminal medical conditions since 2014. On the other hand, large racial disparities in enforcement persist statewide and convictions for cannabis-related infractions have climbed.

Here's a rundown of where Minnesota stands on marijuana's legality:

Minnesota and a greening America

More than half the country, including Minnesota, has legalized marijuana in some form, whether it's for medical treatment in various forms or recreational usage and sales.

Minnesota has one of the nation’s strictest medical marijuana programs, allowing seriously ill patients to ingest concentrated marijuana in edible or vapor form, though prohibiting smoking.

Qualifying conditions include cancer, glaucoma, HIV/AIDS, Tourette Syndrome, ALS, seizures, muscle spasms, Multiple Sclerosis, Chrohn's Disease and any illness deemed terminal.

Cannabis-based treatments are only legally available from twelve licensed dispensaries serving all of Minnesota's 87 counties.

Recreationally, small amounts of marijuana in personal possession have been decriminalized in Minnesota since 1976. Any weight less 42.5 grams or less is a petty misdemeanor on first offense, while future offenses are considered felonies.

Growing revenue

States that have legalized marijuana for either medical or recreational sales have seen boons of additional annual tax revenue (in millions) since instituting those new laws.

Minnesota's tax on medical marijuana sales has so far earned the state an average of $9 million in revenue each year.

However, marijuana is still federally considered a prohibited Schedule One drug, so legal businesses selling cannabis face various operational challenges, such as being required to only accept cash from customers.

Changing attitudes

Both nationally and regionally, support for recreational legalization of cannibis reached about 66 percent last year, according to Gallup surveys.

While acceptance of legalized marijuana has gradually increased nationwide over the decade, support in the midwest has grown more inconsistently, yet still jumped about 25 points from 2009 to 2018.

Source: Gallup | Graphic by Jeff Hargarten, Star Tribune

Previous Star Tribune Minnesota Polls have shown more tepid support, with only 30 percent supporting recreational legalization the last time Minnesota was polled in 2014.

Declining cannabis arrests

The number of marijuana arrests in Minnesota has been steadily declining since 2014, the same year medical cannabis was legalized.

Source: Federal Bureau of Investigation | Graphic by Jeff Hargarten, Star Tribune

Deep racial disparities

Blacks and Native Americans represented disproportionate percentages of cannabis cases filed from 2009 to 2014 in Minnesota, compared to their overall percentage of the population.

Minnesota and Minneapolis have been painted as having among the worst racial disparities in the country, based in part on the prosecution of marijuana cases.

Source: Minnesota Supreme Court | Graphic by Jeff Hargarten, Star Tribune

A 2013 ACLU study showed black people were about 8 times more likely to be arrested for cannabis possession in Minneapolis than white people, even though usage rates are roughly the same.

In 2018, Minneapolis announced the cessation of small-scale marijuana stings after discovering that 46 out of 47 suspects arrested were black.
