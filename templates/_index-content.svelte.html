Marijuana's changing landscape in Minnesota

By Jeff Hargarten and Matt DeLong • Star Tribune

As recreational marijuana gains legal acceptance throughout the nation state-by-state, Minnesota lawmakers are mulling the drug's future.

Gov. Tim Walz has expressed openness to legalizing marijuana for recreational use, following the lead of states like Colorado, Washington, Oregon, California and others.

The state of marijuana in Minnesota is complex and contradictory. On one hand, cannabis has been decriminalized for small doses in Minnesota since the 1970s and available as a treatment for terminal medical conditions since 2015. On the other hand, large racial disparities in enforcement persist statewide and the medical availability of marijuana-based treatments is among the country's most restrictive.

Here's a rundown of where Minnesota stands on marijuana's legality:

The greening of America

More than half the country has legalized marijuana in some form, with a growing number making it available for recreational sales from licensed businesses. Since marijuana is still federally considered a prohibited Schedule One drug, those businesses face various operational challenges. But to states, the additional tax revenue has been a boon for local budgets.

Minnesota's marijuana limitations

Minnesota has one of the nation’s strictest medical marijuana programs, allowing only seriously ill patients to ingest concentrated marijuana in edible or vapor form, but prohibits smoking.

Qualifying conditions include cancer, glaucoma, HIV/AIDS, Tourette Syndrome, ALS, seizures, muscle spasms, Multiple Sclerosis, Chrohn's Disease and any illness deemed terminal.

Cannabis-based treatments are only legally available from eight licensed dispensaries serving all of Minnesota's 87 counties.

Recreationally, small amounts of marijuana in personal possession have been decriminalized in Minnesota since 1976. Any weight less 42.5 grams or less is a petty misdemeanor on first offense, while future offenses are considered felonies.
