Where Minnesota stands on marijuana legalization

By Jeff Hargarten and Matt DeLong • Star Tribune

Updated March 3, 2019

As marijuana use gradually gains legal acceptance throughout the nation, Minnesota lawmakers are mulling the drug's status, with Gov. Tim Walz expressing openness to following the lead of states where recreational cannabis is allowed, regulated and taxed.

Cannabis has been medically legal in Minnesota since 2014, and decriminalized in small doses for decades. Yet, while statewide marijuana arrests have decreased, racial disparities in law enforcement persist and felony convictions have risen.

Here's a rundown of where Minnesota stands on marijuana's legality:

Minnesota and a greening America

Much of the country has legalized cannabis in some form, weather as a medical treatment, as theraputic oils or for recreational usage and sales.

Minnesota has one of the nation’s strictest medical marijuana programs, allowing only the most seriously ill patients to ingest concentrated cannabis in edible or vapor form, while smoking remains prohibited.

Cannabis-based treatments are only legally available from about a dozen licensed dispensaries serving Minnesota's 87 counties, and may only be prescribed for terminal conditions like cancer, glaucoma, HIV/AIDS, Tourette Syndrome, ALS, seizures, muscle spasms, Multiple Sclerosis or Chrohn's Disease.

Recreationally, small amounts (less than 42.5 grams) of marijuana in personal possession have been decriminalized in Minnesota since 1976, with a first offense getting a petty misdemeanor and future infractions being considered felonies.

Growing revenue

The marijuana industries in states with legalized medical or recreational cannabis sales have seen boons of additional annual revenue since the institution of those new laws.

Minnesota's medical marijuana sales have so far generated about $9 million per year.

However, marijuana is still federally considered a prohibited Schedule One drug, so legal businesses selling cannabis face various operational challenges, such as being required to only accept cash from customers.

Changing attitudes

American support for recreational legalization of cannibis reached about 66 percent last year, according to Gallup surveys.

While acceptance of legalized marijuana has gradually increased nationwide over the decade, support in the midwest has grown more inconsistently, yet still jumped about 25 points from 2009 to 2018.

Source: Gallup | Graphic by Jeff Hargarten, Star Tribune

Previous Star Tribune Minnesota Polls have shown more tepid support, with only 30 percent supporting recreational legalization the last time Minnesota was polled in 2014.

Declining arrests, steady convictions

The number of marijuana arrests in Minnesota dropped 16 percent from 2007 to 2017, with the largest decrease following the legalization of medical cannabis in 2014.

Source: FBI, Minnesota Bureau of Criminal Apprehension | Graphic by Jeff Hargarten, Star Tribune

Meanwhile, the number of annual petty misdemeanor and misdemeanor marijuana convictions and remained relatively flat over a six-year period – though felonies gradually increased.

Source: Minnesota Judicial Branch | Graphic by Jeff Hargarten, Star Tribune

Deep racial disparities

Blacks and Native Americans represented disproportionate percentages of sentenced marijuana offenders in 2016 across Minnesota, compared to their overall percentage of the population.

Minnesota and Minneapolis have been painted as having among the worst racial disparities in the country, based in part on the prosecution of marijuana cases.

Source: Minnesota Sentencing Guidlines Commission | Graphic by Jeff Hargarten, Star Tribune

A 2013 ACLU study showed black people were about 8 times more likely to be arrested for cannabis possession in Minneapolis than white people, even though usage rates are roughly the same.

In 2018, Minneapolis announced the cessation of small-scale marijuana stings after discovering that 46 out of 47 suspects arrested were black.
